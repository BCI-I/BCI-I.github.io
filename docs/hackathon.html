What

We are happy to announce MIT's inaugural Brain-Computer Interface (BCI) Hackathon. Participate in this 48-hour event, where teams of 3-5 MIT members will compete to develop the most effective algorithm to control a robot using only their brain.


The hackathon is scheduled for February 2024 at MIT and is free for all MIT members. Winning teams will be eligible for a cash prize.

Why

Brain Computer Interfaces (BCIs) empower direct control of computers and other devices through brain activity. This rapidly expanding field of research finds applications in medicine, entertainment, and beyond. BCIs hold the potential to revolutionize technology interaction and enhance the lives of individuals dealing with neurological disorders and injuries.


Despite remarkable recent progress, substantial work remains for BCIs to become viable in real-world applications. We invite you to contribute to advancing the field of BCI research and development. This hackathon aims to bring together students from diverse backgrounds, fostering learning about BCIs and sparking their interest in this innovative field.
For entrepreneurial participants, winning the hackathon can serve as a significant step towards securing seed funding for your BCI venture.


Where

The hackathon will be held at the McGovern Institute for Brain Research at MIT.
The exact venue information will be announced closer to the date.

When

The hackathon is a 48-hour event and will be held on February 10th & 11th 2024.

How

Competition details

The hackathon adopts a competitive structure where teams have a 48-hour window to develop an algorithm for controlling a robot using only their brain. This physical device control adds an extra layer of complexity compared to software-only challenges, allowing teams to demonstrate their prowess in solving real-world problems.


BCI is a technical field that demands knowledge of neuroscience, signal processing, machine learning, and more. It also relies on specialized hardware for measuring brain activity. To aid your preparation, we offer materials that will equip you to hit the ground running once the competition commences.

Hardware

To enhance accessibility and ensure a level playing field for all teams, identical hardware will be provided. All teams will utilize an X.on EEG headset to record brain activity and a Duckiebot for control. Familiarizing yourself with the materials we've prepared will aid in your preparation for the hackathon.

The X.on EEG headset

Teams will use the X.on wireless EEG headset by Brain Products to record brain activity.

X.on is a new EEG headsets caractterized by its high signal quality, ease of use, and comfort.
It has 7 EEG channels, a ground, an integrated amplifier and the ability to wirelessly stream data through a Bluetooth connection.
You can find additional information on the X.on headset and how to use it for the hackathon here.

The Duckiebot

The DuckieBot

Teams will use the EEG data to control a DuckieBot. Duckiebots are small, affordable, robots by Duckietown, an MIT spin-off company


Duckiebots are designed for testing self-driving algorithms. They have all the hardware necessary to drive autonomously: a front-facing camera, IMUs, time-of-flight sensor. They also pack a brain powerful enough to make sense of all these data (they come with an NVIDIA Jetson Nano 4GB). While Duckiebots are smart enough on their own, we want you to make them even smarter by controlling them with your brain!
You can find additional information on the Duckiebots and how to use them for the hackathon here.
