Hackathon Details

We are happy to announce MIT's inaugural Brain-Computer Interface (BCI) Hackathon. Participate in this 48-hour event, where teams of 3-5 MIT members will compete to develop the most effective algorithm to control a robot using only their brain.


BCI is inherently multidisciplinary, requiring expertise in neuroscience, signal processing, machine learning, and specialized hardware for measuring brain activity. Our goal is to unite students from diverse backgrounds, providing an opportunity to delve into the world of BCI and explore potential collaborative research ventures. Additionally, the winning teams stand a chance to receive small seed funds to kickstart their neurotechnology venture ideas.


What makes this hackathon distinctive is the hands-on challenge where participants are tasked with controlling a physical robot using only their brain. This physical device control introduces an extra layer of complexity, setting it apart from traditional software-only challenges. It not only tests participants' technical skills but also showcases their ability to tackle real-world problems through innovative BCI applications.

This hackathon is organized by the BCI initiative, an organization dedicated to promoting education and research in BCI. How to participate

The hackathon will be held in late February at the McGovern Institute at MIT. Signup will open in January 2024 and participation will be open to all MIT members and is free. Make your interest known by filling up this form.
Ahead of the hackathon, we will release material to help you familiarize yourself with the hardware and software used during the hackathon.

Hardware

To enhance accessibility and ensure a level playing field for all teams, identical hardware will be provided. All teams will utilize an X.on EEG headset to record brain activity and a Duckiebots for control.

X.on EEG headset

The X.on wireless EEG headset by Brain Products has 7 EEG channels for high-quality EEG recordings.

Duckiebot

Duckiebots are affordable self-driving cars designed by Duckietown to promote education on self-driving vehicles.

Competition

The hackathon adopts a competitive structure where teams have a 48-hour window to develop an algorithm for controlling a robot using only their brain.


At the end of the competition, teams will present their participate in a live demonstration of their algorithm to determine the winners.

Teams

Teams must consist of 3-5 MIT members. We encourage participants to form teams prior to the hackathon. Participants without a team will be assigned to one at the beginning of the event.

Prizes

Winning teams will be provided with the opportunity to participate in the MIT Sandbox Innovation Fund Program. MIT Sandbox plays a pivotal role in entrepreneurship education, extending essential funding and mentorship to empower MIT student innovators. Selected teams will gain access to the program, receiving mentorship along with a small budget to prototype their venture ideas.


Sandbox generously offers three prizes:
 First place    - $1,500
 Second place - $1,000
 Third place   - $500

Participating in Sandbox

Participation in MIT Sandbox's program involves specific requirements: each team must have at least one MIT student in a leadership role and participation is conditional on proposing a venture project. Furthermore, the proposed venture project must align with Sandbox's mission and guidelines, reflecting a commitment to the values and goals of MIT Sandbox, emphasizing innovation and entrepreneurship. Teams seeking sponsorship approval must submit a venture project application, subject to a final review by MIT Sandbox.

Cash prizes

For those teams that do not meet the criteria or choose not to enroll in the Sandbox program, we provide an alternative option — a cash prize of $500 (max 2 teams).
